The Deep Crisis Beneath Our Feet: Haryana’s Vanishing Water Table

How decades of policy, climate, and inequality are pushing India’s breadbasket toward “Day Zero”

By Aditi Mital • Rohtak, Haryana

Published on: • 8 min read

A dried-up well in Karnal district. Groundwater levels have dropped over 2 meters per year. Credit: All Rounder Field Archive / 2025

Contents

    ROHTAK, HARYANA — In the village of Nissing, 45 kilometers from the state capital, 68-year-old farmer Balbir Singh hasn’t seen his tube well yield water before 3 a.m. in over five years. “We used to irrigate three crops,” he says, wiping sweat under a relentless April sun. “Now we pray for one.”

    Haryana, once the crown jewel of India’s Green Revolution, is running out of water. With over 76% of its blocks classified as “over-exploited” by the Central Ground Water Board, the state faces a slow-motion emergency that threatens food security, rural livelihoods, and intergenerational equity.

    The Green Revolution Legacy

    Launched in the 1960s, the Green Revolution transformed Haryana into a wheat and rice powerhouse. But it came with a hidden cost: water-intensive crops, subsidized electricity for pumps, and no regulation on extraction.

    “We fed the nation, but we drank our future dry.”

    Data: The Numbers Behind the Crisis

    According to CGWB 2024 data:

    • Average annual groundwater decline: 1.8 meters
    • Blocks in “critical” or “over-exploited” category: 89 out of 119
    • Rice cultivation occupies ~40% of cropped area
    Groundwater depth in key Haryana districts (2000–2025). Source: CGWB

    Local Impact: Haryana on the Brink

    In Mewat and Rewari, women walk up to 3 kilometers daily for drinking water. In Sirsa, schools report absenteeism during summer as children help families fetch water.

    Policy Failures and Pathways Forward

    Despite schemes like “Jal Shakti Abhiyan,” enforcement remains weak. Experts advocate for:

    • Shifting from rice to millets and pulses
    • Real-time groundwater monitoring
    • Community-led water governance

    Why It Matters

    Haryana’s crisis is a warning for India and the world. If the breadbasket fails, national food security unravels. But if it succeeds in sustainable transition, it becomes a global model.

    2. Structural Crisis: Global Water Security

    Detailed, long-form journalism on natural resources has lasting impact because the crisis is slow-moving and structural. We zoom in on specific regions to tell universal stories.

    “Day Zero” in Haryana

    While Cape Town made headlines, Haryana’s “Day Zero” is already here — silently. In 2023, 12 villages in Mahendragarh went 112 days without piped water. Unlike Chennai, where protests erupted, Haryana’s crisis is absorbed by migration: young men leave for Gurugram, leaving women and elders to manage scarcity. This quiet exodus masks a deeper truth: water stress is reshaping Haryana’s demography.

    The Politics of Rivers

    The Yamuna, once Haryana’s lifeline, now carries more sewage than freshwater. Downstream, Delhi’s demands override upstream needs. Meanwhile, the Indus Waters Treaty — signed in 1960 — is fraying under climate stress. A new dam in Himachal could reduce flow to Haryana’s Sirsa by 30%. Water is no longer just a resource; it’s a weapon of federal negotiation.

    Why It’s Timeless

    Water is fundamental. A report on a past drought serves as a chilling, essential warning for every future one.

    3. The Enduring Power of Inequality and Caste

    The most enduring stories dissect the mechanisms of power that persist across generations.

    Wealth Disparity in Haryana

    Haryana has India’s second-highest per capita income — and its worst sex ratio. This paradox reveals a deeper truth: wealth concentrates among dominant castes, while marginalized communities face systemic exclusion. Land records from Rohtak show that 78% of agricultural land is held by just two Jat clans. Meanwhile, Dalit entrepreneurs in Faridabad struggle to secure bank loans despite identical business plans.

    The Shadow of Caste

    In a three-generation study across Karnal, we tracked two families: one Jat, one Dalit. Both started with similar assets in 1980. By 2025, the Jat family owned 40 acres and a tractor dealership. The Dalit family, despite higher education levels, remained landless laborers. Caste isn’t just social — it’s economic infrastructure.

    Why It’s Timeless

    These stories are about the foundational structures of society. While names and dates change, the pattern of exclusion remains.

    4. The Science of (Near) Immortality

    Billionaires and biologists are racing to extend “healthspan” — but at what cost to society?

    The Biologists and Billionaires

    In labs funded by tech moguls, senolytics clear “zombie cells” that cause aging. CRISPR edits genes linked to longevity. But these therapies cost millions. In Gurgaon, a startup offers “longevity consultations” for ₹5 lakh/year — accessible only to the elite. Meanwhile, Haryana’s public hospitals struggle to provide basic geriatric care.

    The Pension Crisis

    If Haryanvi farmers live to 120, who funds their retirement? India’s pension coverage is below 15%. A 100-year career would require re-skilling every decade — a luxury rural youth can’t afford. “Biological inequality” could become the ultimate caste system.

    The Meaning of Life

    If death is no longer guaranteed, does life lose urgency? In interviews with Haryana’s elderly, many say mortality gives life meaning. “If I live forever,” said 82-year-old Sita Devi of Hisar, “why plant a tree today?”

    5. Undersea Cables & Invisible Infrastructure

    The internet is not “in the cloud” — it runs through 1.3 million kilometers of undersea fiber, much of it owned by Google, Meta, and Amazon.

    Chapter 1: The Invisible Infrastructure

    The first transatlantic cable (1858) took 16 days to send a message. Today, a cable from Mumbai to Marseille carries 224 terabits per second — enough for 40 million Zoom calls. Laying it cost $500 million and took 3 years. Haryana’s IT hubs in Panchkula depend entirely on this fragile web.

    Chapter 2: The Soft Underbelly

    95% of global data flows through just 200 landing stations. One in Mumbai handles 70% of India’s international traffic. In 2023, a Chinese research vessel was spotted near the Andaman cable route. Naval experts warn: cutting three cables could blackout India’s digital economy for weeks.

    Chapter 3: The New Imperialism

    Google’s “Equiano” cable bypasses Europe, linking Portugal directly to South Africa — reshaping African internet sovereignty. In the Indian Ocean, China’s “Digital Silk Road” competes with U.S.-backed projects. Small island nations like Mauritius are pressured to host landing stations — becoming pawns in a new cold war.

    Why It Matters for Haryana

    When a farmer in Bhiwani checks crop prices on his phone, that data travels through cables laid by foreign corporations, guarded by foreign navies. Digital sovereignty starts with understanding the wires beneath the waves.
